Most Beautiful by Ruskin Bond – A Detailed Three-Page Explanation

Introduction

“Most Beautiful” is a short story by Ruskin Bond featured in his semi-autobiographical Rusty series, specifically in Rusty Comes Home. Bond, celebrated for his nuanced storytelling and exploration of human emotions, dives into the themes of social cruelty, mental health, innocence, empathy, and the tragic search for acceptance in this story. It captures the emotional journey between the narrator and a mentally challenged boy named Suresh, examining how fragile beauty and connection can be in a world that often fails to understand or care for the vulnerable.

Page 1: Plot Overview and Characters

The story begins when the narrator, likely a younger version of Rusty, encounters a disturbing scene on a quiet afternoon. A group of children are tormenting a mentally challenged and physically deformed boy around thirteen years old by throwing stones and mocking him. The boy is defenseless and unable to articulate his pain due to his condition. Outraged by the cruelty, the narrator intervenes, scolding the bullies and taking the boy to his home. The boy communicates only through gestures.

Upon reaching his home, a beautiful and kind woman in her mid-thirties opens the door. She immediately understands what has happened and expresses gratitude. She reveals that the boy is her son, Suresh, and though she cares for him, his father is disappointed in him and emotionally distant. When the father arrives, his lack of warmth towards Suresh is evident, but he appreciates the narrator’s kindness.

Main Characters:
  • The Narrator (Rusty): A sensitive and empathetic young man who forms a bond with Suresh and reflects deeply on his experiences.
  • Suresh: A mentally challenged and physically deformed boy. Though unable to communicate well, his emotional world is deep and complicated.
  • Suresh’s Mother: Warm, gentle, and hopeful, she sees potential in the positive influence the narrator has on her son.
  • Suresh’s Father: Cold, practical, and disappointed in his son. He represents societal rejection of those who don’t conform to conventional standards.

Page 2: Development of the Bond and Psychological Layers

Over time, the narrator becomes a regular visitor at Suresh’s home. He often takes Suresh on walks through the nearby hills, forests, and streams. These outings are joyful for the boy, who becomes increasingly attached to the narrator. Suresh even learns how to swim during one of these trips. These moments showcase the healing power of nature and human connection.

One day, a baby goat (referred to as a “kid”) begins following the two of them. Suresh takes a liking to the animal, bringing it home and playing with it. However, when others begin to praise the kid’s cuteness, Suresh feels overshadowed and insignificant. Unable to handle the lack of attention, he kills the kid using a brick.

This horrifying act reveals the darker aspects of Suresh’s mind. It shows how his need for attention, emotional instability, and inability to express or process jealousy manifest in destructive behavior. When the narrator confronts him, Suresh smiles—acknowledging his deed. He then offers the narrator a pen-knife and gestures him to stab him. The narrator is shocked but hides his reaction with laughter and throws the knife into a stream.

This moment reveals both Suresh’s guilt and his struggle to understand love and punishment. It’s a powerful, symbolic moment indicating how people with mental challenges often internalize rejection and trauma in unpredictable ways.

Page 3: The Farewell, Symbolism, and Conclusion

Eventually, the narrator informs the family that he is leaving for Delhi, possibly for a long time. The mother is visibly saddened because she has seen how her son was beginning to open up and improve. However, Suresh himself shows no visible emotion or reaction. This coldness unsettles the narrator, making him wonder whether their bond had any real impact.

But on the day of departure, at the train station, just before the train leaves, the narrator sees Suresh in the crowd. He calls out to him, and Suresh, in a rare display of urgency and emotion, runs toward the train. Unfortunately, he stumbles over someone’s luggage and falls. The narrator watches helplessly as Suresh lies on the platform, surrounded by people, immersed in darkness. That is the last time the narrator ever sees him.

Themes and Symbolism:
  • Cruelty of Society: The story opens with a disturbing example of how society treats those who are different. Suresh is mocked and isolated.
  • Mental Illness and Innocence: Suresh’s actions, while sometimes disturbing, are rooted in his struggle to understand and be understood.
  • Empathy and Connection: The narrator’s genuine efforts to befriend and help Suresh show how small acts of kindness can offer meaning, even if briefly.
  • Jealousy and Identity: The killing of the goat reveals Suresh’s internal conflicts with being overlooked or unloved.
  • Loss and Memory: The train departure scene symbolizes the narrator's irreversible parting from a brief, meaningful connection that forever changed him.

Conclusion

“Most Beautiful” is a haunting yet beautiful short story that challenges traditional notions of beauty, normalcy, and love. It explores how people with mental disabilities are misunderstood and mistreated, but also how they long for affection and recognition like anyone else. Through Suresh, Ruskin Bond paints a portrait of silent suffering, emotional turbulence, and the desperate human need to belong. The story leaves readers with a sense of helplessness, guilt, and sorrow—but also with a deep respect for the complexity of the human mind and heart.
