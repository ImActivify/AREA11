Father Returning Home By Dilip Chitre

By Dilip Chitre (ISC Class 11 Level)

Introduction

Dilip Chitre’s poem Father Returning Home is a vivid, melancholic portrayal of an aged man's alienation in a bustling, modern society. Through sparse, precise imagery and an emotionally detached narrative, the poet draws attention to the tragic invisibility of the elderly—especially fathers—who often become silent, background figures in the families they once helped shape.

In this free verse poem, Chitre writes about his father’s routine commute home from work and his passive, almost ghost-like existence within his family. However, the poem is far more than just a description of mundane events—it is a profound social commentary on aging, disconnection in urban life, and the emotional vacuum within modern families.

About the Poet: Dilip Chitre

Dilip Chitre (1938–2009) was a significant voice in Indian poetry, especially in the post-independence era. A bilingual poet writing in both Marathi and English, Chitre was also a translator, painter, and filmmaker. His poetry often explores themes of alienation, cultural dislocation, urban chaos, and spiritual emptiness, reflecting his concerns about modernity and identity.

Poem Overview

Father Returning Home is written in two stanzas, both free of rhyme or consistent meter. The first stanza describes the father’s physical journey on a commuter train. The second focuses on his psychological journey—his experience at home, his internal state, and his disconnection from the world around him.

Line-by-Line and Stanza-by-Stanza Analysis

Stanza 1: The Commute

My father travels on the late evening train
Standing among silent commuters in the yellow light
Suburbs slide past his unseeing eyes
His shirt and pants are soggy and his black raincoat
Stained with mud and his bag stuffed with books
Is falling apart. His eyes dimmed by age
Fade homeward through the humid monsoon night.

This stanza sets the tone of urban melancholy. The father is on a late evening train, symbolizing both the physical end of the day and metaphorically, the final stages of his life. He stands “among silent commuters,” showing that he is surrounded by people, yet deeply alone.

Now I can see him getting off the train
Like a word dropped from a long sentence.
He hurries across the length of the grey platform,
Crosses the railway line, enters the lane,
His chappals are sticky with mud, but he hurries onward.

Here, the speaker imagines the father disembarking. The simile “like a word dropped from a long sentence” is incredibly evocative. It implies that the father is no longer part of a meaningful narrative; he is forgotten, insignificant.

Stanza 2: The Home and Inner Life

Home again, I see him drinking weak tea,
Eating a stale chapati, reading a book
He goes into the toilet to contemplate
Man’s estrangement from a man-made world.

This stanza shifts from the external world to the internal and emotional state of the father. It begins with mundane actions—drinking weak tea, eating stale chapati—to illustrate the emptiness of his domestic life.

Coming out he trembles at the sink,
The cold water running over his brown hands,
A few droplets cling to the graying hair on his wrists,
His sullen children have often refused to share
Jokes and secrets with him. He will now go to sleep
Listening to the static on the radio, dreaming
Of his ancestors and grandchildren, thinking
Of nomads entering the subcontinent through a narrow pass.

These lines show the physical fragility of the father—he trembles as he washes his hands. The father is not just neglected; he is excluded from the bonding and emotional life of his children.

Major Themes

  • Alienation in Urban Life: Despite being in crowds, the father is emotionally isolated.
  • Breakdown of Family Dynamics: The children are indifferent, the father is excluded.
  • Emotional and Physical Decay: Stale food, soggy clothes, trembling hands — symbols of deterioration.
  • Search for Identity and Belonging: Dreams of ancestors and nomads reflect a deeper yearning for purpose and connection.
  • Existential Reflection: “Man’s estrangement from a man-made world” hints at Chitre’s philosophical concerns.

Poetic Devices

  • Imagery: “Yellow light,” “mud-stained raincoat,” and “static on the radio” create a sensory and emotional landscape.
  • Symbolism: Train = mechanical life, Static = emptiness, Books = fading identity.
  • Simile: “Like a word dropped from a long sentence” conveys loss of relevance.
  • Enjambment: Unstructured thoughts mirror his fractured emotional world.

Relevance Today

With increasing urbanization and generation gaps, this poem is more relevant than ever. It calls on the youth to recognize emotional neglect and rebuild familial bonds before it’s too late.

Conclusion

Father Returning Home by Dilip Chitre is a small poem with enormous emotional and philosophical depth. It is a mirror to society—reminding us to acknowledge the silent suffering of elders and to find humanity in our fast-paced, indifferent lives.
